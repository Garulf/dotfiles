# Composition & Framing

Composition is controlling where the viewer's eye lands and travels. Everything below is a lever on that.

## Visual weight

The eye is pulled hardest by, in rough order: human faces and eyes, high-contrast boundaries (bright against dark), saturated color (especially warm), large masses, and anything touching a frame edge or corner. "Heavy" elements demand attention proportional to these factors, not their story importance — a saturated red trash can will beat a pale protagonist.

Practical rules:

- Keep heavy elements away from edges and corners unless they're deliberately counterweighting something.
- Darker objects, bright zones, and warm saturated colors are heavier than pale, cool, or mid-tone ones.
- Living forms (faces, figures, animals) carry psychological weight beyond their size; even a tiny figure dominates a landscape.

## Balance

- **Static balance** — similar objects, equidistant from center: calm, harmony, formality.
- **Dynamic balance** — dissimilar objects at different distances from center, equilibrium through size/position trade-offs: energy while staying stable. A large near mass balanced by a small far one.
- **Deliberate imbalance** — asymmetry with no counterweight: tension, unease, expressiveness. Use on purpose, never by accident.
- The classic move: main subject on one third, a lesser secondary element opposite to keep the frame from tipping.

## Placement systems

- **Rule of thirds** — subject on a third-line or intersection. The default that beats centering for anything non-symmetrical.
- **Golden ratio / phi grid / Fibonacci spiral** — slightly tighter placement than thirds; spiral suggests an eye path curling into the subject.
- **Golden triangles** — corner-to-corner diagonal plus perpendiculars from the other corners; place elements in the resulting triangles. Best for diagonal-dominated scenes.
- **Centered + symmetry** — only when the scene IS symmetric (reflections, architecture, ritual formality — the Wes Anderson look). Centering a non-symmetric subject reads static and amateur.
- **Rule of odds** — 3 or 5 subjects group naturally; even counts split into pairs and read static unless visually grouped.
- **Rule of space** — moving or gazing subjects need empty space *ahead* of the motion/gaze, not behind.
- **Left-to-right rule** — Western viewers scan left→right; motion flowing that way feels progressive, against it feels obstructed.

## Eye path

1. Decide the primary subject — the eye's destination.
2. Build a route to it: leading lines (roads, fences, walls), implied lines (a gaze, a pointing arm, 2–3 objects the eye connects), diagonals and S-curves.
3. Place secondary elements at grid intersections as waypoints.
4. Audit for leaks: lines that exit the frame drag the eye with them; lines that split the frame in half kill it. Distracting bright spots at edges hijack the path.

Gestalt principles underneath this: **continuation** (the eye follows a started path), **figure-ground** (subject must separate from background via contrast of value, saturation, or sharpness), **similarity** (like elements group — use for unity), **proximity** (near elements read as related — use for storytelling clusters), **closure** (viewers complete partial shapes — implied framing works).

## Depth

Flat images bore. Layer them:

- **Three planes** — foreground interest, midground subject, background context. Two planes when they'd compete; one plane only for deliberately graphic scenes (fog, patterns).
- **Linear perspective** — converging lines, strongest entering from frame edges/corners.
- **Atmospheric (aerial) perspective** — distance desaturates, lightens, and lowers contrast. Backlight amplifies haze; frontal light kills it.
- **Tonal perspective** — dark foreground framing a brighter distance reads as depth even without haze.
- **Overlap** — objects occluding each other is the cheapest depth cue there is.

## Space and framing devices

- **Frame within a frame** — arches, windows, branches, doorways around the subject: adds depth layers and locks attention inward.
- **Fill the frame** — subject dominates, minimal surround: intensity, intimacy.
- **Negative space** — generous emptiness around the subject: isolation, scale, breathing room, elegance. The deliberate opposite of fill-the-frame.
- **Juxtaposition** — contrasting/complementary elements side by side to tell a story (old/new, big/small).
- **Background discipline** — background gives context but must never merge with the subject (watch for poles "growing" from heads, clutter at similar value to the subject).
- **Break the pattern** — a repeating texture/pattern with one interruption; the interruption becomes the subject.

## Aspect ratio and cropping

- Match orientation to the dominant lines: horizontal frames for horizontal flow, vertical for vertical subjects.
- Square is maximally stable — reserve for symmetry and pattern; it deadens dynamic scenes.
- Panoramic/ultrawide (wallpapers): put the horizon nearer center than thirds would suggest, or foreground overweights; let the width carry layered vistas.
- Never crop *at* a joint (knees, wrists, neck) — crop between joints. Don't amputate curve shapes at the frame edge if the curve is the point.

## Common mistakes

- Centered horizon splitting the frame; centered subject with no symmetry to justify it
- Bright/sharp distractions at edges and corners; subject crowded against an edge with no breathing room
- Background clutter at the same value/saturation as the subject (figure-ground failure)
- Even-numbered ungrouped subjects; every element the same size (no hierarchy)
- No foreground in a landscape (depth collapses); lines leading out of frame

## In prompts

- Placement: `on the right third of the frame`, `subject small in the lower left, vast empty sky above` (negative space + scale), `centered symmetrical composition, one-point perspective` (only for symmetry).
- Eye path: `a winding path leads from the foreground to...`, `she gazes toward the left, open space ahead of her`.
- Depth: `layered composition with foreground rocks, midground figure, distant mountains`, `atmospheric haze softening the far ridges`, `framed through an archway`.
- Energy: `dynamic diagonal composition`, `sweeping S-curve river`; calm: `balanced horizontal composition, still water symmetry`.
- Anti-clutter: `clean uncluttered background`, `minimalist composition, generous negative space`.
- AI models default to centered, symmetrical, subject-at-50% framing — explicitly ask for off-center placement, foreground layers, and directional gaze/motion space or you won't get them.
